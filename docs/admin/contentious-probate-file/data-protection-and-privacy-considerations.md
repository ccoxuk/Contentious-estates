## Overview

Data protection is a fundamental legal and ethical obligation. Contentious probate matters involve sensitive personal information requiring careful handling under the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and Data Protection Act 2018.

---

## Legal Framework

### Key Legislation

**General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)**
- Applies from 25 May 2018 (retained in UK law post-Brexit)
- Sets principles for processing personal data
- Grants rights to data subjects
- Imposes obligations on data controllers
- Significant penalties for breach (up to Â£17.5m or 4% of global turnover)

**Data Protection Act 2018**
- UK implementation of GDPR
- Supplements GDPR
- Specific provisions for legal services

**Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations (PECR)**
- Regulates electronic marketing
- Cookie consent requirements

### Regulatory Guidance

**Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)**
- UK supervisory authority
- Issues guidance and codes of practice
- Investigates complaints
- Imposes penalties

**Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA)**
- SRA Standards and Regulations require compliance with data protection laws
- Professional duty of confidentiality overlaps with data protection

## Key Definitions

### Personal Data

**Definition:**
Information relating to an identified or identifiable living individual

**In Contentious Probate Includes:**
- Client's name, address, contact details
- Beneficiaries' details
- Family members' information
- Medical information about deceased (if relates to living individuals)
- Financial information
- Witness information
- Correspondence

**Note:** Information about deceased persons is NOT personal data under GDPR (deceased have no data protection rights). However:
- Medical records may mention living relatives
- Professional courtesy and client confidentiality still apply
- Family members may be data subjects

---

### Special Category Data

**Definition:**
Personal data revealing:
- Racial or ethnic origin
- Political opinions
- Religious or philosophical beliefs
- Trade union membership
- Genetic data
- Biometric data
- Health data
- Sex life or sexual orientation

**In Contentious Probate:**
- Medical information (capacity issues)
- Health conditions of living parties
- Family relationships (may reveal sexual orientation)
- Mental health information

**Enhanced Protection:**
- Stricter conditions for processing
- Explicit consent often required
- Enhanced security measures necessary

---

### Data Controller

**Definition:**
Entity determining purposes and means of processing

**In Practice:**
- Your law firm is the data controller
- Responsible for GDPR compliance
- Liable for breaches
- Must implement appropriate safeguards

---

### Data Processor

**Definition:**
Entity processing data on controller's behalf

**Examples:**
- IT service providers
- Cloud storage providers
- Transcription services
- Document scanning services
- Counsel (may be processor or joint controller)

**Requirements:**
- Written contract (data processing agreement)
- Processor must provide sufficient guarantees
- Specific contractual terms required by GDPR

## GDPR Principles

### Six Key Principles

Personal data must be:

**1. Lawfully, Fairly, and Transparently Processed**
- Lawful basis for processing
- Inform data subjects how data will be used
- No hidden processing

**2. Collected for Specified, Explicit, and Legitimate Purposes**
- Clear purpose for collecting data
- Don't use for incompatible purposes
- In legal practice: providing legal services, complying with legal obligations

**3. Adequate, Relevant, and Limited (Data Minimization)**
- Only collect necessary information
- Don't collect "just in case"
- Review what data you actually need

**4. Accurate and Up to Date**
- Take reasonable steps to ensure accuracy
- Correct inaccurate data promptly
- Allow data subjects to update their information

**5. Kept No Longer Than Necessary (Storage Limitation)**
- Retention schedules
- Delete/destroy when no longer needed
- Balance with other legal obligations (professional indemnity, limitation periods)

**6. Processed Securely (Integrity and Confidentiality)**
- Appropriate technical and organizational measures
- Protect against unauthorized processing, loss, destruction
- Security proportionate to risk

## Lawful Basis for Processing

### Six Lawful Bases (GDPR Article 6)

**For Contentious Probate, Typically:**

**1. Legitimate Interests (Article 6(1)(f))**
- Your firm's legitimate interest in providing legal services
- Must conduct balancing test (interests vs data subject's rights)
- Document your assessment

**2. Contract (Article 6(1)(b))**
- Processing necessary to perform contract with client
- Taking steps at client's request before entering contract

**3. Legal Obligation (Article 6(1)(c))**
- Compliance with legal obligations (e.g., money laundering regulations, court orders)
- Professional conduct requirements

**Less Common:**

**4. Consent (Article 6(1)(a))**
- Not typically relied upon for legal services
- Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous
- Can be withdrawn
- Avoid relying on consent for essential processing

**5. Vital Interests (Article 6(1)(d))**
- Rarely applicable in legal context

**6. Public Task (Article 6(1)(e))**
- Not applicable to private law firms

---

### Special Category Data

**Requires BOTH:**
- Lawful basis under Article 6 (above), AND
- Condition under Article 9

**Article 9 Conditions (Commonly Used):**

**Explicit Consent (Article 9(2)(a))**
- Higher standard than regular consent
- Must be explicit
- Document carefully

**Legal Claims (Article 9(2)(f))**
- Processing necessary for establishment, exercise, or defense of legal claims
- Most relevant for contentious probate
- Covers litigation and pre-litigation

**Substantial Public Interest (Article 9(2)(g) + DPA 2018)**
- Various conditions in DPA 2018 Schedule 1
- May apply to legal services

## Client Information and Consent

### Privacy Notice

**Requirement:**
- Provide privacy notice to data subjects
- Explain how personal data will be used

**Timing:**
- At point of collecting data
- Before processing begins

**Content (Article 13/14):**
- Identity of data controller
- Contact details of data protection officer (if applicable)
- Purposes of processing
- Lawful basis
- Recipients of data
- Retention periods
- Data subject rights
- Right to complain to ICO
- Information about automated decision-making (if any)

**Format:**
- Clear and plain language
- Easily accessible
- Often provided with client care letter
- Can be layered (brief notice + full privacy policy)

---

### Consent (When Required)

**When Needed:**
- Processing not covered by other lawful bases
- Special category data (unless legal claims exemption applies)
- Marketing communications
- Certain disclosures outside necessary processing

**Valid Consent Requires:**
- Freely given (real choice, no detriment for refusal)
- Specific (to particular purpose)
- Informed (understand what consenting to)
- Unambiguous (clear affirmative action)
- Documented
- Can be withdrawn at any time

**Obtaining Consent:**
- Separate from other terms and conditions
- Granular (separate consents for different purposes)
- Easy to withdraw
- Record when, how, what data subject told

## Data Subject Rights

### Individual Rights Under GDPR

**1. Right to be Informed**
- Transparency about processing
- Privacy notice

**2. Right of Access (Subject Access Request - SAR)**
- Obtain copy of their personal data
- Respond within one month
- Usually free
- Verify identity

**3. Right to Rectification**
- Correct inaccurate data
- Complete incomplete data
- Respond within one month

**4. Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten")**
- Limited circumstances
- Not absolute - many exemptions
- Legal obligations to retain may override

**5. Right to Restrict Processing**
- Limit what you do with data
- Specific circumstances

**6. Right to Data Portability**
- Receive data in structured format
- Limited application in legal context

**7. Right to Object**
- Object to processing based on legitimate interests
- Must stop unless compelling legitimate grounds
- Always stop direct marketing if objected to

**8. Rights Related to Automated Decision-Making**
- Not to be subject to solely automated decisions
- Rarely applicable in legal practice

---

### Responding to Subject Access Requests

**Process:**

1. **Verify Identity**
   - Ensure requester is data subject or authorized
   - Request ID if not already verified

2. **Clarify Request**
   - What data does person want?
   - Which time period?

3. **Search for Data**
   - Electronic and paper files
   - Emails
   - Case management systems
   - Archived data

4. **Consider Exemptions**
   - Legal professional privilege
   - Third-party information
   - Other exemptions in DPA 2018

5. **Redact Where Necessary**
   - Remove privileged information
   - Remove third-party data (if not reasonable to disclose)
   - Explain redactions

6. **Provide Response**
   - Within one month
   - Extend by two months if complex (inform requester)
   - Usually free
   - Clear copies of data

**Exemptions:**
- Legal professional privilege applies
- May withhold privileged legal advice
- May withhold in certain circumstances if would prejudice legal proceedings

## Security Measures

### Technical Measures

**Access Controls:**
- Password protection
- Multi-factor authentication
- User access levels (need-to-know)
- Audit trails

**Encryption:**
- Encrypt sensitive data at rest
- Encrypt data in transit (email encryption, secure file transfer)
- Encrypt devices (laptops, phones, USB drives)
- Encrypted cloud storage

**Backup and Recovery:**
- Regular backups
- Secure backup storage
- Test recovery procedures
- Business continuity planning

**IT Security:**
- Firewalls
- Anti-virus software
- Regular updates and patches
- Secure networks
- VPNs for remote access

- Data protection policy
- Information security policy
- Clear desk/clear screen policy
- Incident response plan
- Data retention policy

**Training:**
- Regular staff training
- Awareness of data protection
- Specific training for high-risk processing
- Ongoing refresher training

**Physical Security:**
- Secure building access
- Locked filing cabinets
- Secure storage for sensitive files
- Shredding confidential waste
- Visitor management

**Third-Party Management:**
- Vet suppliers (data processors)

- Monitor compliance

## Data Sharing

### When Sharing is Necessary

**In Contentious Probate:**
- With client
- With opponent/opponent's solicitors
- With court
- With counsel
- With experts
- With third parties (medical professionals, financial institutions)

**Considerations:**
- Is sharing necessary?
- Lawful basis for sharing?
- Minimum necessary information?
- Secure transmission?
- Inform data subjects (privacy notice)?

---

### Disclosure to Opponents

**Litigation Context:**
- Disclosure is part of litigation process
- Legal claims exemption may apply
- Still ensure secure transmission

**Redaction:**
- Redact irrelevant personal data where appropriate
- Redact third-party data not necessary to disclose
- Balance with duty of disclosure

---

### Court Documents

**Public Documents:**
- Court bundles may be public
- Sensitive information may need to be redacted or anonymized
- Apply for restrictions if necessary (e.g., initials instead of names)

---

### Counsel and Experts

**Data Processors or Joint Controllers:**
- Assess relationship (likely joint controllers for counsel, processors for some experts)
- Data sharing agreement or understanding
- Ensure counsel/experts have appropriate security

**Instructions:**
- Only provide necessary information
- Secure transmission
- Remind of confidentiality obligations

---

### International Transfers

**Transfers Outside UK:**
- Stricter rules for transfers to countries outside UK
- Need adequacy decision or appropriate safeguards
- Relevant if foreign clients, counsel, experts, parties

**Safeguards:**
- Standard contractual clauses
- Binding corporate rules
- Derogations (e.g., explicit consent, legal claims)

## Data Breaches

### What is a Data Breach?

**Definition:**
Breach of security leading to accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorized disclosure of, or access to, personal data

**Examples:**
- Email sent to wrong recipient
- Lost laptop with unencrypted data
- Ransomware attack
- Unauthorized access to files
- Lost paper file
- Inadvertent disclosure in court documents

---

### Breach Response

**Immediate Steps:**

1. **Contain**
   - Stop the breach
   - Recover data/devices if possible
   - Prevent further loss

2. **Assess**
   - What data involved?
   - How many data subjects?
   - Sensitivity of data?
   - Potential harm?

3. **Notify Internally**
   - Inform senior management
   - Inform data protection officer (if you have one)
   - Document the breach

4. **Notify ICO (If Required)**
   - Within 72 hours if "likely to result in a risk to rights and freedoms"
   - Use ICO's online reporting tool
   - Provide details of breach, data involved, likely consequences, mitigation

5. **Notify Data Subjects (If Required)**
   - If "likely to result in a high risk to rights and freedoms"
   - Without undue delay
   - Clear, plain language
   - Advise on steps they can take

6. **Mitigate**
   - Take steps to reduce harm
   - Offer support to affected individuals
   - Remedy vulnerabilities

7. **Document**
   - Record breach in breach register
   - Document all steps taken
   - Learn lessons and improve procedures

---

### Breach Register

**Requirement:**
- Maintain record of all breaches (even those not reported to ICO)
- Include facts, effects, remedial action

**Purpose:**
- Demonstrate accountability
- Learn from incidents
- ICO may request to see

## Retention and Disposal

### Retention Periods

**General Principle:**
Keep only as long as necessary for the purpose

**Legal Practice:**
- Minimum retention often dictated by professional indemnity insurance (typically 6-7 years from closure)
- Limitation periods
- Regulatory requirements
- SRA guidance: minimum 6 years

**Contentious Probate:**
- Typical retention: 6-15 years from matter closure
- Longer for high-value/complex matters
- Permanent retention for some documents (wills, certain orders)

**Retention Policy:**
- Document retention periods for different document types
- Review and update regularly
- Balance retention needs with data minimization principle

---

### Secure Disposal

**When Destroying Data:**

**Physical Documents:**
- Shred or incinerate
- Use confidential waste service
- Don't simply bin
- Certificate of destruction

**Electronic Data:**
- Permanent deletion (not just recycle bin)
- Degaussing or physical destruction of hard drives
- Wipe devices before disposal
- Ensure backups also deleted

**Third-Party Disposal:**
- Use reputable disposal company
- Data processing agreement
- Obtain certificate of destruction

## Special Situations

### Deceased Data Subjects

**GDPR Does Not Apply:**
- Deceased have no data protection rights
- But confidentiality and professional courtesy remain

**Living Relatives:**
- Information about deceased may relate to living persons (medical information, family relationships)
- Those living persons have data protection rights

---

### Vulnerable Clients

**Enhanced Care:**
- Vulnerable clients may need additional support understanding privacy information
- Take extra care with consent
- Consider capacity issues
- Appoint representative if necessary

---

### Children

**Under 18:**
- Can give consent for online services at age 13+ (in UK)
- For other processing, depends on child's maturity/understanding
- Parental consent may be required
- Take age-appropriate measures

**In Contentious Probate:**
- Beneficiaries may be children
- Handle their data appropriately
- Consider parental involvement

## Practical Guidance for Fee Earners

### Day-to-Day Compliance

**DO:**
- Provide privacy notice to clients
- Only collect necessary information
- Store data securely (lock files, encrypt emails)
- Send emails to correct recipients (check before sending)
- Redact unnecessary personal data when disclosing
- Delete/destroy data when no longer needed
- Report data breaches immediately
- Undertake data protection training

**DON'T:**
- Email sensitive data unencrypted
- Leave files unattended
- Discuss cases in public places
- Access data you don't need
- Keep data "just in case"
- Ignore subject access requests
- Assume someone else will deal with it
