## Overview

Contentious probate encompasses disputes arising from the administration of estates and the validity of wills. This section provides essential background for practitioners handling such matters.

---

## What is Contentious Probate?

Contentious probate refers to litigation concerning:
- The validity of wills (want of knowledge and approval, lack of testamentary capacity, undue influence, fraud, forgery)
- Interpretation of wills and trusts
- Claims under the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975
- Removal or substitution of executors or administrators
- Disputes concerning the administration of estates
- Professional negligence claims against executors or estate administrators

---

## Common Types of Disputes

### 1. Validity Challenges
- **Testamentary Capacity**: Did the testator have sufficient mental capacity?
- **Knowledge and Approval**: Did the testator know and approve the will's contents?
- **Undue Influence**: Was the testator coerced or unduly influenced?
- **Fraud and Forgery**: Is the will genuine and properly executed?
- **Want of Due Execution**: Was the will properly witnessed and executed per s.9 Wills Act 1837?

### 2. Inheritance Act Claims
- Claims by spouses, civil partners, children, and dependants
- Reasonable financial provision from the estate
- Time limits and procedural requirements

### 3. Proprietary Estoppel and Constructive Trusts
- Promises and assurances regarding inheritance
- Detrimental reliance
- Equity and unconscionability

### 4. Administration Disputes
- Executor misconduct or breach of fiduciary duty
- Removal of executors or administrators
- Passing of executor's or administrator's accounts

## Jurisdiction and Court Procedures

### Business & Property Courts (Chancery Division)

Most contentious probate matters are heard in the Chancery Division of the High Court, specifically within the Business and Property Courts structure.

**Key Points:**
- Claims typically issued in the High Court
- Some matters may be dealt with in County Court depending on value and complexity
- Specialist judges with expertise in trust and probate law

### Relevant Court Rules and Practice Directions
- Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) Part 57 (Probate and Inheritance)
- CPR Part 8 (Alternative procedure for claims)
- CPR Part 7 (Standard procedure)
- Practice Direction 57
- Chancery Guide

## Key Legislation

### Primary Legislation
- **Wills Act 1837**: Requirements for valid will execution
- **Administration of Estates Act 1925**: Estate administration framework
- **Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975**: Family provision claims
- **Trustee Act 1925 & 2000**: Trustee powers and duties
- **Senior Courts Act 1981**: Court jurisdiction and powers

### Secondary Legislation
- Non-Contentious Probate Rules 1987
- Relevant statutory instruments and amendments

## Professional Conduct Considerations

### Conflicts of Interest
- Identify potential conflicts early
- Consider whether you can act for executor and beneficiary simultaneously
- Document conflict checks and client consents

### Confidentiality and Privilege
- Maintain strict confidentiality
- Understand legal professional privilege boundaries
- Handle sensitive family information appropriately

### Costs and Funding
- Provide clear costs information
- Consider conditional fee agreements where appropriate
- Be aware of costs budgeting requirements
- Advise on after-the-event insurance

### Regulatory Requirements
- SRA Standards and Regulations
- Money laundering regulations
- Data protection compliance (GDPR)

## Preliminary Considerations

Before accepting instructions:

1. **Jurisdiction**: Confirm the matter falls within contentious probate
2. **Limitation**: Check limitation periods haven't expired
3. **Conflict**: Complete thorough conflict checks
4. **Funding**: Discuss costs and funding options
5. **Merits**: Conduct preliminary merits assessment
6. **Alternatives**: Consider mediation or alternative dispute resolution

## Key Deadlines and Limitation Periods

| Claim Type | Limitation Period | Notes |
|------------|-------------------|-------|
| Inheritance Act 1975 claims | 6 months from grant of representation | Court may grant permission for late claims |
| Validity challenges | No strict limitation, but laches applies | Act promptly; delay may bar relief |
| Proprietary estoppel | Variable, typically 12 years | Depends on nature of claim |
| Fraud/fraudulent concealment | 6 years from discovery | Different rules apply |
| Estate accounts | 6 years (contract), 12 years (specialty) | From cause of action arising |

**Note**: Always verify current limitation periods and seek specialist advice where necessary.
